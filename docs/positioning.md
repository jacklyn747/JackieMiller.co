# Positioning Strategy — "AI for Work You Sign Your Name To"

*Session notes, July 13–14, 2026. This is the strategy record for all site copy. Start here.*

## The decision

Jackie's expertise is framed as: **setting up AI systems for people who do high-stakes work** — work where the output has to be factually correct because being wrong has a named cost. This covers the whole current portfolio:

- **Legal research** — a wrong citation or a lost fact means sanctions, dismissal, a career mark.
- **Documentary film research** — a wrong claim means retraction and a discredited film.
- **Clinical nutrition program** (friend's pilot at a weight-loss clinic, no GLP-1 doctor on site) — a wrong recommendation means real harm to a real person.

**Verdict from research: the strategy is right; the phrase "high-stakes" should not be the headline.**

## What the research found

The gap Jackie identified is real and measurable — it is the best-documented finding in AI adoption right now:

- OpenAI's own workplace data shows most users paste a paragraph in for a quick edit; structured, reliable AI workflows stay concentrated among a tiny set of power users. Access isn't the problem — crossing the bridge is.
- Udacity research: 9 in 10 workers use AI; 3 in 4 have abandoned a task because they couldn't trust the output. **Trust in output is the bottleneck, not adoption.**
- Stack Overflow 2025: even developers — the most AI-fluent profession — show trust *falling* as usage rises (distrust of accuracy jumped 31% → 46% in one year).
- Legal: 700+ court cases involve AI-fabricated content; 1,400+ cases where courts addressed AI errors; real sanctions and fines ($31k against two firms in California). Every lawyer prospect has heard of a colleague getting burned.
- The generic "AI automation agency" market is saturated (template sellers, launch-and-leave shops). Specific problem + defined customer beats generalist positioning on pricing and sales effort.

## Why "high-stakes" can't be the headline

1. **It's already corporate wallpaper.** Wolters Kluwer runs "high-stakes professional decision-making" as its Expert AI tagline; FTI Consulting and Salesforce use the phrase too. A solo consultant can't own language a billion-dollar company is spending on.
2. **It doesn't filter.** Jackie's own insight: *everybody* thinks their work is high stakes. If everyone self-identifies in, the phrase selects nobody. Define stakes by **consequence**, not importance: "if this is wrong, something specific and bad happens."
3. **Never promise "right." Promise "checkable."** Nobody can make AI incapable of being wrong, and the first client who catches an error owns your reputation. What Jackie actually sells: systems where every output is grounded in sources, verifiable, and passes human sign-off before it counts. This also keeps her out of liability trouble in the legal and health verticals.

## Language direction (in order of confidence)

1. **"AI for work you sign your name to."** — Recommended lead. Self-selects perfectly (the lawyer signing a filing, the filmmaker in the credits, the coach whose face is on the program). Implies accountability without claiming perfection. Nobody owns this language.
2. **"For work that has to survive being checked."** — More muscular; strong for legal/documentary where someone adversarial *will* check.
3. **"When 'mostly right' isn't good enough."** — Supporting line only; too negative to lead.
4. Avoid "accuracy-first" / "verified AI" phrasing — reads like compliance software, not a person you hire.

Buyer language to mirror in copy: clients don't say "I need high-stakes AI systems." They say **"I can't put my name on what it gives me."**

## Site copy structure

1. **Name the gap** — you use AI for emails, not for the work that pays you; here's why that's rational, not timid.
2. **Name the cost of the gap** — the people who bridged it carelessly got sanctioned; the people who never bridge it fall behind.
3. **The offer** — systems with verification built in, tuned to your actual work.
4. **Proof** — the three case studies (legal, documentary, clinical nutrition). Three non-overlapping verticals is not a focus problem; the through-line is the method, and the variety proves the method transfers.

## The anchor case study: State v. Courtine Walker (Dallas County, 2026)

A capital murder case (double homicide, pending since 2023) dismissed on speedy-trial grounds after Judge Nancy Mulder called the state's conduct "apathy, languid and insolent" and "a dereliction of prosecutorial duty." What the prosecution did wrong:

- Refused to test ballistics and cell phone data until court-ordered.
- Told the defense they didn't have one of the guns when they did (lead prosecutor James Worley later admitted the error).
- The case-killer: Worley could not tell the judge when he had last spoken to the **sole eyewitness**. From the bench: *"You are the lead prosecutor on this multiple capital murder case. He is the only eyewitness and you cannot tell the court the last time you spoke to him?"*

Walker had been jailed 3+ years since age 19; he remains held on a separate aggravated robbery charge; the DA has appealed the dismissal. Sources: WFAA coverage (article + video), Yahoo News syndication.

**Why this story is the pitch:** the failure wasn't incompetence — it was *entropy*. A competent-enough person managing too many moving parts with memory and a calendar. Every failure was a failure to know the state of his own case file, not a failure of intelligence. That is every prospective client. Write it up as: real failure → named cost → the system that would have made it impossible. See [system-architecture.md](system-architecture.md) for the system we designed against it.
